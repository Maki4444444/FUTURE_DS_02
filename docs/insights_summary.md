# Insights Summary

## Executive Summary

Analysis of customer churn reveals that the company's retention challenge is not isolated to a specific customer segment, pricing tier, or engagement level. Instead, churn is driven by multiple factors affecting the customer experience across the entire product. Approximately **20.8% of Monthly Recurring Revenue (MRR)** is currently lost to churn, representing a significant opportunity for revenue recovery through targeted retention initiatives.

---

## Key Insights

### 1. Churn Is a Product-Wide Issue Rather Than a Pricing Problem

**Observation:**
Enterprise, Pro, and Basic customers all exhibit nearly identical churn rates of approximately **22%**.

**Why It Matters:**
The similarity across plan tiers indicates that churn is not primarily driven by pricing or customer size. Even Enterprise customers, who typically receive onboarding support and account management, are leaving at the same rate as lower-tier customers, suggesting broader issues with product value or customer experience.

**Recommendation:**
Prioritize product improvements and customer success initiatives instead of plan-specific discounts or retention campaigns.

---

### 2. Customer Engagement Is Not a Reliable Predictor of Churn

**Observation:**
Retained and churned customers have nearly identical engagement score distributions, with average scores differing by only **2.4 points**.

**Why It Matters:**
Highly engaged customers are still cancelling their subscriptions, indicating that product usage alone is not sufficient to prevent churn. Customers are likely leaving due to unmet business needs, missing functionality, competitive alternatives, or budget constraints.

**Recommendation:**
Shift focus from engagement-triggered campaigns toward proactive customer relationship management, structured feedback collection, and stronger alignment between customer feedback and product development.

---

### 3. Churn Is Driven by Multiple Factors

**Observation:**
No single churn reason dominates. Feature limitations, support issues, budget constraints, competitor offerings, pricing concerns, and unknown reasons all contribute at similar levels.

**Why It Matters:**
Because churn is broadly distributed, solving only one problem will have limited impact. Additionally, **16%** of churn events are classified as **Unknown**, limiting the organization's ability to understand customer departures.

**Recommendation:**
Implement a cross-functional retention strategy involving Product, Customer Support, and Sales while making exit surveys mandatory to reduce unknown churn reasons.

---

### 4. Recent Customer Cohorts Show Higher Retention

**Observation:**
Customers acquired during late 2024 demonstrate substantially higher retention than earlier cohorts, while the February 2023 cohort shows unusually poor retention.

**Why It Matters:**
The apparent improvement may indicate product enhancements, but it may also reflect insufficient time for newer customers to reach their renewal period. The February 2023 cohort should be investigated to identify any unique contributing factors.

**Recommendation:**
Continue monitoring cohort performance over the next 6–12 months and perform a detailed analysis of the February 2023 cohort to identify potential root causes.

---

### 5. Churn Represents a Significant Revenue Opportunity

**Observation:**
The business loses approximately **$2.35 million in Monthly Recurring Revenue**, equivalent to **20.8%** of total MRR, due to customer churn.

**Why It Matters:**
Even modest improvements in retention could generate substantial financial returns. Recovering only **20%** of lost MRR would return approximately **$471,000** in recurring monthly revenue, easily justifying investments in customer success, onboarding improvements, and win-back programs.

**Recommendation:**
Position revenue recovery as the primary business objective for retention initiatives and use the MRR loss metric as the headline KPI in executive dashboards and stakeholder presentations.

---

## Overall Conclusion

The analysis indicates that customer churn is a company-wide challenge rather than a problem confined to a particular pricing tier or engagement level. Since churn is distributed across multiple causes, no single intervention will significantly reduce customer loss. A coordinated strategy combining product enhancements, improved customer support, structured feedback collection, and proactive customer success efforts offers the greatest opportunity to improve retention.

With over **$2.35 million in monthly recurring revenue** currently lost to churn, even incremental reductions in churn can deliver substantial business value and long-term revenue growth.
